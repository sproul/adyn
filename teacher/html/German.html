du/ihr vs. Sie

The English word you translates to the German words Sie or du (plural Ihr).
  • Sie is used in formal situations.
  • Sie is used in situations where you are addressing more than one person.
  • Du is used when addressing a close friend.
  • Du is used when speaking to a child.
  • Du can be used when speaking from a position of authority to a subordinate.
  • Ihr is the plural form of du.
Note that all of the second person formal pronouns are capitalized when used to address directly (e.g., in a letter), and that no others are.

Strong and Weak Verbs

Modal Auxiliary Verbs

Modal verbs express a context for other verbs, telling whether they are possible or desirable. There are six modal verbs:

dürfen be allowed to
können be able to
mögen like
müssen have to
sollen be expected to
wollen want

When a modal auxiliary verb is conjugated in a perfect tense with an infinitive, instead of using the standard past participle as one would normally expect, the modal auxiliary verb's infinitive serves as the past participle instead. This same practice is also used with the verbs sehen, hören, and lassen.

Sie konnten Japanisch sprechen.
They [feminine] could [past tense] speak Japanese.

Ihr habt spazieren können.
You [familiar, plural] have been able to walk.

Infinitives following modal verbs are not preceded by the customary zu.

Separated Verbs

There is a class of verbs in German which have separable prefixes. In the present and imperfect tenses, these verbs are split into their components. An example of such a verb is vollbringen (to achieve):

Wir bringen voll.
We achieve.

Ich brachte voll.
I used to achieve.

In other tenses these verbs do not split.

Sie wird vollbringen.
She will achieve.

Negative Statements

German statements are negated by using the word nicht, which corresponds to the English word not.
  • Nicht precedes predicate adjectives, nouns, pronouns and adverbs.
  • Nicht precedes expressions of time.
  • Nicht precedes prepositional phrases.
  • Nicht precedes past participles.
  • Nicht precedes infinitives.
  • Nicht precedes separable prefixes.
  • Nicht precedes the main verb in a dependent clause.
  • If a contrast is being drawn between two entities, nicht may for emphasis directly precede the item which is being negated.

Imperative -- forming commands

Simply omit the personal pronouns to form a command.

Reflexive Verbs

A reflexive verb expresses an action executed on the verb's subject, which is also represented in the sentence by a reflexive pronoun.

This grammatical style is used only occasionally in English, e.g., he helped himself, but is used more often in German.

The Perfect Tense

Events which took place in the past are described using the perfect tense. In German this tense is constructed using a helper verb, normally haben, along with the past participle of the verb describing what happened. This is analogous to the use of the verb to have for the same purpose in English.

Forming the Past Participle

For weak verbs one forms the past participle by prefixing ge- and replacing the trailing -en with t:

Sie haben eine Couch gekauft.
You [formal, plural] have bought a couch.

For separated verbs one forms the past participle in the same manner as for weak verbs except the leading ge- is placed after the separation:

Sie brachten voll.
They achieved.

This tense is used to describe events which happened in the past.

The Perfect Tense vs. The Imperfect Tense

The difference in meaning between the perfect tense and the imperfect tense is very subtle. The imperfect tense is more common in the written language, and is slightly more likely to be used when referring to an action which was repeated in the past. But these tenses are somewhat interchangeable. This presents a problem for translation exercise software; if their meaning is so similar, how can the student know which tense to use? The convention that I adopted was to mapped completed past tense phrases (e.g., "I ran" or "I have run") to the German perfect tense, and repeated past tense phrases (e.g., "I used to run") to the German imperfect tense. It is important to note that the distinction is not so strong in the language itself; I just needed a firm rule to guide the choice of tenses in the exercises.

The convention adopted in the exercises was

  • to map completed past tense phrases with no helping verb (e.g., "I ran") to the German imperfect tense,
  • to map completed past tense phrases with a helping verb (e.g., "I have run") to the German perfect tense,
  • and repeated past tense phrases (e.g., "I used to run") to the German imperfect tense.

Conditional Tense

The conditional perfect tense is used to describe events which would or should happen. It is formed by attaching the imperfect tense endings to the future tense stem.

Future Perfect Tense

The future perfect tense is used to describe events which will have happened in the future. It is formed by conjugating the auxiliary verb in the future tense, and adding the past participle of the action verb.

Pluperfect Tense

The pluperfect tense is used to describe events which had happened in the past. It is formed by conjugating the auxiliary verb in the imperfect tense, and adding the past participle of the action verb.

Past Conditional Tense

The past conditional tense is used to describe events which would have happened in the past. It is formed by conjugating the auxiliary verb in the conditional tense, and adding the past participle of the action verb.

Konjuntiv I (Subjunctive Mood)

The subjunctive moods are frequently a source of confusion for English speakers because their use in German does not always have a direct equivalent in English. It is hard to sum up their purpose without oversimplifying, but the common thread underlying their use is the expression of information with some doubt added. It is probably best for the student to initially focus less on precisely translating its meaning than on developing a sense for the various situations in German where its use is called for. Broadly speaking, the subjunctive is used to imply doubt or to express that the reported fact may not be true, and is used to describe indirectly reported speech.

Konjuntiv II (Past Subjunctive Mood)

In German, this tense is equivalent to the conditional tense, where this tense is known as the simple form (because there is no helping verb) and the conditional tense is known as the compound form (since the conditional form of werden is used as a helping verb). In most cases one can substitute either for the other.

Some Verbs Require Konjuntiv II to Express "Conditional" Meaning

There is an important exception to the general rule of equivalence between the conditional and Konjunctiv II tenses mentioned above, however: the modal auxiliary verbs ( dürfen , können , mögen , müssen , sollen , and wollen ) and the verbs sein , haben , and werden always use the simple form (i.e., Konjunctiv II ). Although a German speaker would certainly understand the meaning intended by a combination of one of these verbs with würde, this usage would be considered awkward.

Konjuntiv I vs. Konjuntiv II

Although the traditional names of these tenses subjunctive and past subjunctive imply a time relationship between them analogous to that between the present and the perfect tenses, in fact this is misleading. Both of these forms refer to the present. For this reason more recent German grammatical texts refer to these tenses by new names which do not imply a time relationship: Konjunctiv I and Konjunctiv II .

Past Konjuntiv II (Pluperfect Subjunctive Mood)

In German, this tense is equivalent in meaning to the past conditional tense, and in most cases one can substitute either for the other. However, there is a set of verbs which require using the Konjunctiv II tenses intead of the würde constructions.

Verbs which take the Dative

Although normally the dative case indicates an indirect object, there is a class of verbs in German for which one expresses objects which appeared to be direct in the dative case. This is unfortunately another situation where there are no hard and fast rules allowing the student to identify which verbs take the dative and which don't. However, verbs with the prefixes bei-, ent-, entgegen-, nach-, wider-, or zu- typically take the dative. Also, a characteristic of dative verbs which is fairly common is that the receiver of the action is often considered to have either been helped or hurt by the action.

Case

In German, every noun and adjective has a case, which indicates its role in the sentence. There are four cases in German:
  • accusative: the accusative case indicates a direct object
  • genitive: the genitive case indicates ownership
  • dative: the dative case indicates an indirect object
  • nominative: the nominative case indicates the subject
Nouns, adjectives, articles, and demonstrative adjectives all have various endings which depend on case.

Articles

Every German object has a gender: either masculine or feminine or neuter. Depending on an object's use in a sentence, it will also have a case: either nominative, accusative, genitive, or dative.

Nouns

Just as German determiners vary by case, so do, in some cases, German nouns.
  • in the genitive case neuter nouns take a -s or -es ending.
  • similarly, strong masculine nouns take a -s or -es ending in the genitive case only.
  • weak masculine nouns take an -en ending in all cases except for the nominative.
Unfortunately, there is no way to tell by inspection whether a masculine noun is weak or strong.

-s vs. -es

  • If the noun ends with -el, -em, -en, or -er, then only the -s ending is appropriate.
  • If the noun ends with -s, -ß, -sch, -z, -zt then only the -es ending is appropriate.
German nouns are always capitalized.

Forming Plurals

German nouns of foreign origin form the plural by adding -s, just as an English. These nouns do not vary by case in the singular.

Other German nouns add -n or -en in the dative case in the plural. For other cases there are a number of tendencies but no surefire rules governing how the plural is formed.

Adjectives

Predicate adjectives, i.e., adjectives used with linking verbs like sein, finden, haben and werden, do not take any endings. But German adjectives used in other situations normally change according to the gender, number and case of the object or objects they refer to. How they change depends on the immediate context of their use; there are three different declensions which apply, as detailed below.

Adjectives After Indefinite Pronouns

When an adjective is used after an indefinite pronoun (e.g., etwas, viel, wenig, etc.), it is always capitalized.

Declension #1: Weak Endings

When an adjective is preceded by a definite article or demonstrative adjective, it takes weak endings:

nominative accusative dative genitive
masculine singular -e -en -en -en
feminine singular -e -e -en -en
neuter singular -e -e -en -en
plural -en -en -en -en

Declension #2: Mixed Endings

When an adjective is preceded by an indefinite article or possessive pronoun, it takes mixed endings:

nominative accusative dative genitive
masculine singular -er -en -en -en
feminine singular -e -e -en -en
neuter singular -es -es -en -en
plural -en -en -en -en

Declension #3: Strong Endings

When an adjective is used in situations not covered by the first two declensions, it takes strong endings:

nominative accusative dative genitive
masculine singular -er -en -em -en
feminine singular -e -e -er -er
neuter singular -es -es -em -en
plural -e -e -en -er

How to memorize the adjectival declensions

Above we saw that there are three situations, which when multiplied by the four cases (nominative, accusative, genitive, and dative) and then multiplied again by the possibilities for gender and number, give us 48 endings to memorize. But in fact it's not so hard as that because of the similarity between the three sets of endings. I think the easiest way to approach the problem is to construct a table of the most "common" endings, which will form a base for further study. If you perform this exercise, you will see that the resulting table is identical to the table of mixed endings. So the first step is to memorize the mixed endings. Then the next step is to memorize only the endings which are different in the other two situations, which are shown below. Thus the number of items which must be absorbed is drastically reduced.
