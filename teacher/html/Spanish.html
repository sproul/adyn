tù/vosotros versus usted

The English word you translates to the Spanish words usted (plural ustedes) or tù (plural vosotros, feminine plural vosotras).
  • Usted is used in formal situations.
  • Usted is used in situations where you are addressing more than one person.
  • Tù is used when addressing a close friend.
  • Tù is used when speaking to a child.
  • Tù can be used when speaking from a position of authority to a subordinate.

Present Tense

The present tense is used to describe events which are happening now. In Romance languages like Spanish there is no distinct equivalent to the English progressive verb form, e.g., both I speak and I am speaking are expressed in Spanish as hablo.

ser and estar: To Be

These irregular verbs are among the most fundamental of the language.
  • Use estar (not ser) to describe changeable characteristics or mood.
  • Use ser (not estar) to describe permanent characteristics.
  • Note that feliz is considered to be an enduring condition, and so is normally used with ser (which is an exception to the rule that estar be used to describe changeable characteristics or mood).
  • Use estar instead of ser to indicate locations, even if they are permanent.

    Él está en Londres.
    He is in London.

  • listo can be used with either estar or ser, but means ready when used with estar, and clever when used with ser.
  • Use ser to indicate where something or someone is from.

Reflexive Verbs

A reflexive verb expresses an action executed on the verb's subject, which is also represented in the sentence by a reflexive pronoun.

This grammatical style is used only occasionally in English, e.g., he helped himself, but is used much more often in Spanish.

Nosotros nos gozamos tanto.
We are enjoying ourselves very much.

Me llamo John.
I'm called John.

Lucho contra la injusticia.
I am fighting against injustice.

Es posible lastimarse en ese lugar.
It is possible to hurt oneself at that place.

Cepillamos los dientes cada noche antes de acostarnos.
We brush our teeth each night before going to bed.

Past Tense

Events which took place in the past are described using the preterite or the past tense. In Spanish the past tense is constructed using a helper verb, normally haber, along with the past participle of the verb describing what happened. This is analogous to the use of the verb to have for the same purpose in English.

Conditional Tense

The conditional perfect tense is used to describe events which would or should happen. It is formed by attaching the imperfect tense endings to the future tense stem.

Future Perfect Tense

The future perfect tense is used to describe events which will have happened in the future. It is formed by conjugating the auxiliary verb in the future tense, and adding the past participle of the action verb.

Pluperfect Tense

The pluperfect tense is used to describe events which had happened in the past. It is formed by conjugating the auxiliary verb in the imperfect tense, and adding the past participle of the action verb.

Adjectives

In Spanish adjectives do not have a single form, as they do in English. Spanish adjectives must agree with their objects in gender and number.
  • Adjectives ending with the letter o change to end with the letter a in their feminine forms.

    Yo fui egoísta.
    I was selfish.

  • Adjectives ending with iz change to end with ices in their plural forms.

    Ellas fueron inteligentes.
    They [feminine] were intelligent.

Articles

In Spanish, every object has a gender: either masculine or feminine.

Definite Articles

Spanish speakers translate the English word the several ways depending on the gender and number of the object(s) being referred to:

singular singular before a vowel plural
masculine el el los
feminine la el las

la is replaced by el when immediately preceding a noun beginning with a stressed a sound. Spanish requires the definite article before names of languages, except after the verbs hablar and, usually, aprender.

Nosotros hablamos inglés.
We spoke English.

Spanish requires the definite article before nouns describing abstractions or substances in general. Spanish requires the definite article before each noun in a list.

Indefinite Articles

Spanish speakers translate the English words a and some several ways depending on the gender and number of the object(s) being referred to:

singular singular before a vowel plural
masculine un un unos
feminine una un unas

una is replaced by un when immediately preceding a noun beginning with a stressed a sound. When expressing the concept some meaning a few with respect to a substance or to an imprecise number of countable items, Spanish speakers just omit the article altogether. When expressing the concept some meaning a small number of with respect to a countable noun, Spanish speakers use unos (or unas for feminine nouns). When expressing the concept some meaning several but not all with respect to a precise (even if implicit) quantity of some countable noun, Spanish speakers use algunos (or algunas for feminine nouns). When expressing the concept some with respect to a substance or to an imprecise number of countable items, Spanish speakers just omit the article altogether. When expressing the concept a with respect to an object which has no singular or is referred to in pairs (like, in English, pants), Spanish speakers use unos (or unas for feminine nouns).
