tu/voi versus Lei

The English word you translates to the Italian words Lei (plural Loro) or tu (plural voi).
  • Lei is used in formal situations.
  • Lei is used in situations where you are addressing more than one person.
  • Tu is used when addressing a close friend.
  • Tu is used when speaking to a child.
  • Tu can be used when speaking from a position of authority to a subordinate.

Present Tense

The present tense is used to describe events which are happening now. In Romance languages like Italian there is no distinct equivalent to the English progressive verb form, e.g., both I speak and I am speaking are expressed in Italian as parlo.

Reflexive Verbs

A reflexive verb expresses an action executed on the verb's subject, which is also represented in the sentence by a reflexive pronoun.

This grammatical style is used only occasionally in English, e.g., he helped himself, but is used much more often in Italian.

Ci piacciamo molto.
We are enjoying ourselves very much.

Sono chiamato John.
I'm called John.

Lotto contro l'ingiustizia.
I am fighting against injustice.

È possibile per farsi male a quel luogo.
It is possible to hurt oneself at that place.

Spazzoliamo nostri denti ogni notte prima d'andare a letto.
We brush our teeth each night before going to bed.

Present Perfect

Events which took place in the past are described using the present perfect. In Italian this past tense is constructed using a helper verb, normally avere, along with the past participle of the verb describing what happened. This is analogous to the use of the verb to have for the same purpose in English.

Forming the Past Participle

  • the past participle for an -are verb is formed by adding -ato to its stem
  • the past participle for an -ere verb is formed by adding -uto to its stem
  • the past participle for an -ire verb is formed by adding -ito to its stem

Forming the Past Using essere

Some Italian verbs use avere as their helper verb to express the past, while others use essere instead.
  • reflexive verbs are conjugated with essere
  • most intransitive verbs are conjugated with essere. This is especially true of those verbs describing
    • movement or
    • some process of physical or psychological change.
  • verbs indicating atmospheric conditions may be conjugated with either avere or essere
  • transitive verbs with direct objects are conjugated with avere
When forming the past using essere, the past participle must agree with the subject. Thus,
  • if the subject is feminine, a past participle normally ending with to should end with ta

    Lei andò al ristorante.
    She went to the restaurant.

  • if the subject is feminine and plural, a past participle normally ending with to should end with te
  • if the subject is masculine and plural, a past participle normally ending with to should end with ti

Direct Objects which Precede the Past Participle

When any of the direct object pronouns lo, la, li, or le precedes the past participle, that past participle is treated as if it were an adjective modifying the direct object (or objects). Thus the usual rules regarding agreement between adjectives and their objects in gender and number must be observed.

Lei ha colputo i bambini. Lei l'ha colputi.
She beat the children. She beat them.

Lui ha alzato le ragazze per che loro possono vedere. Loro l'ha alzate.
He lifted the girls up to allow them to see. He lifted them up.

When the direct object pronoun ne is used to mean some or some portion of, the past participle is treated as if it were an adjective modifying the direct objects. Thus the usual rules regarding agreement between adjectives and their objects in gender and number must be observed.

Past Definite

Events which occurred in the past and have completely finished are sometimes described using the past definite instead of the present perfect; this is very common in the written language. Italians in Central and southern Italy are prone to using the past definite in the spoken language as well; northern Italians are much less likely to do so. Alternate Forms for -ere Verbs

These verbs have alternate forms for the first person singular and third person forms of this tense:
  • etti instead of ei
  • ette instead of é
  • ettero instead of erono

Imperfect Tense

The imperfect tense is used to describe repeated or extended action in the past. This is in contrast to the past definite and present perfect, which are much more likely to describe individual events in the past.

...che Lei compri un cappotto.
...that you [formal, singular] buy [subjunctive] a coat.

Io avevo comprato una macchina fotografica.
I had bought a camera.

Lei andrebbe lí con loro?
Would you [formal, singular] go there with them?

Quest'estate spero d'andare in Francia.
I hope to go to France this summer.

Future Tense

The future tense is used to describe events which will happen in the future. This tense is formed by appending the future tense endings to the future tense stem, which is usually the infinitive:

Conditional Tense

The conditional perfect tense is used to describe events which would or should happen. It is formed by attaching the imperfect tense endings to the future tense stem.

Future Perfect Tense

The future perfect tense is used to describe events which will have happened in the future. It is formed by conjugating the auxiliary verb in the future tense, and adding the past participle of the action verb.

Pluperfect Tense

The pluperfect tense is used to describe events which had happened in the past. It is formed by conjugating the auxiliary verb in the imperfect tense, and adding the past participle of the action verb.

Subjunctive Mood

The subjunctive mood is frequently a problem area for English speakers because its use in Italian generally does not have a direct equivalent in English. It is used to express doubt and emotion, but it is probably best for the student to initially focus less on precisely translating its meaning than on developing a sense for the various situations in Italian where its use is obligatory. Below are some of these situations:
  • The subjunctive mood is required in dependent clauses where the main clause expresses a desire.

    Desidero che lei sia qui.
    I wish that she were here.

    Io voglio che lei sappia che io l'ho ingannata.
    I want her to know that I tricked her.

  • The subjunctive mood is required in dependent clauses where the main clause expresses a command.

    Ho comandato che questo crimine succede.
    I commanded that this crime take place.

  • The subjunctive mood is required in dependent clauses where the main clause expresses a doubt or denial.

    Dubitiamo che lo possiamo fare.
    We doubt that we can do it.

    Lei nega che suo marito sia brutto.
    She denies that her husband is ugly.

  • The subjunctive mood is required in dependent clauses where the main clause expresses fear.

    Temono che il ragazzo si perda.
    They fear that the boy will get lost.

  • The subjunctive mood is required in dependent clauses where the main clause expresses emotions like joy or sorrow.

    Sono cosí felice che sono morti!
    I am so happy that they are dead!

  • The subjunctive mood is required in dependent clauses where the main clause expresses a hope.

    Tutti sperano che quest'uomo parte.
    We all hope that this man leaves.

  • The subjunctive mood is required in dependent clauses where the main clause expresses a superlative statement.

    Questa è l'ultima volta che Lei me vedrà!
    This is the last time that you [singular, formal] will see me!

    Ciò è la donna più bella che mai ho visto!
    That's the most beautiful woman I have ever seen!

  • The subjunctive mood is required in dependent clauses where the main clause is an impersonal expression expressing uncertainty, necessity, or appropriateness.

    Lei deve venire.
    You [formal, singular] must come.

    È meglio che non sa.
    It is better that he doesn't know.

    Questo è il momento justo per fare dal proprio meglio.
    It is time that we tried our best.

    È possibile che veniamo questa sera.
    It is possible that we come this evening.

    È giusto che ammette i suoi errori.
    It is right that he admit his errors.

    Sembra che siamo le vittime.
    It seems that we are the victims.

    È raro che ognuno vede cosí uccello qui.
    It is rare that one sees such a bird here.

    È raro che ognuno vede cosí uccello qui.
    It is rare that one sees such a bird here.

    È buono che arriviamo cosí prima.
    It is good that we arrive so early.

    È sfortunato che non siamo più intelligente.
    It is unfortunate that we are not more intelligent.

    È importante che possa imparare a vivere solo.
    It is important that she be able to learn to live alone.

    But:
    The indicative, not the subjunctive, is appropriate in dependent clauses where the main clause is an impersonal expression expressing certainty or substantial probability.

    È chiaro che è stupido.
    It is clear that he is stupid.

    È certo che arriveremo in tempo.
    It is certain that we will arrive in time.

    Dependent clauses where the main clause is an impersonal expression expressing certainty or substantial probability require the subjunctive if the statement is negative or interrogative.

    È chiaro che è stupido?
    Is it clear that he is stupid?

    È certo che arriveremo in tempo?
    Is it certain that we will arrive in time?

  • The subjunctive is obligatory in independent clauses expressing a command in the third person. Normally this style is the province of people blessed with tremendous authority, e.g., kings.

    Cue loro sian tranquilli!
    Let them be quiet!

Articles

Every Italian object has a gender: either masculine or feminine.

Definite Articles

Italian speakers translate the English word the several ways depending on both the gender of the object being referred to and the spelling of its beginning. The different combinations are covered in the following table:

singular singular before vowel singular before s and consonant or z plural plural before vowel plural before s and consonant or z
masculine il l' lo i gli lo
feminine la l' la le le le

The definite article is used when describing a category or type of object in general. Note that in English no article of all is used in this particular situation.

lo and gli

lo and gli are used to indicate masculine nouns
  • beginning with z or
  • beginning with the combination of s and another consonant, or
  • gn-, ps-, pn-, x-
  • y- and a vowel, or i- and a vowel.
The definite article is used with names of countries, regions, and named geological features. The definite article is not used with unmodified names of countries and regions when described as the destination in a phrase with andare. The definite article is used with names of countries and regions used with andare if the name is modified by an adjective. With a handful of exceptions, unmodified names of cities are not accompanied by the definite article. Names of cities which are modified by adjectives must be accompanied by the definite article. The names of languages must be accompanied by the definite article, except when used immediately neighboring forms of the verb parlare. Expressions of time must be accompanied by the definite article, except mezzogiorno (noon) and mezzonotte (midnight).

Normally the definite article is required in addition to the possessive determiner.

Write your name on the wall, please.

When a singular relative is referred to by any possessive determiner except loro, no definite article is required.

Dormiva nel seminterrato di sua madre finché era degli anni di trentacinque vecchio.
He was sleeping in his mother's basement until he was thirty-five years old.

When relatives are referred to by the possessive determiner loro, the definite article is always required. This is true even for singular references to relatives (which can normally be referred to with a possessive determiner unaccompanied by the definite article).

Forming Plurals for Nouns and Adjectives

  • Adjectives and masculine nouns which end with -o or -a simply change the final letter to -i.
  • Adjectives and nouns ending with -e also change the final letter to -i.
  • Adjectives and feminine nouns ending with -a, however, change the final letter to -e.
  • Adjectives which and with -io where the i sound is stressed change the final letter to i.
  • Adjectives which and with -io where the i sound is not stressed drop the final o.
Note that there are some exceptions to these rules.

Adjectives

In Italian adjectives do not have a single form, as they do in English. Italian adjectives must agree with their objects in gender and number. Adjectives ending with the letter o change to end with the letter a in their feminine forms.

Io fui egoistico.
I was selfish.

Loro sarebbero stati storditi.
They would have been dizzy.

The masculine plural form ends with the letter i:

Tu sei sgarbato.
You [familiar, singular] are rude.

Feminine plural adjectives end with e:

Voi siete stati grati.
You [familiar, plural] have been grateful.
