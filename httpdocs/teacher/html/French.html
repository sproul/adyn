tu versus vous

The English word you translates to the French words vous or tu.
  • Vous is used in formal situations.
  • Vous is used in situations where you are addressing more than one person. Note that this includes situations where you are addressing more than one person informally.
  • Tu is used when addressing a close friend.
  • Tu is used when speaking to a child.
  • Tu can be used when speaking from a position of authority to a subordinate.

Present Tense

The present tense is used to describe events which are happening now. In Romance languages like French there is no distinct equivalent to the English progressive verb form, e.g., both I speak and I am speaking are expressed in French as je parle.

-er Verbs

Verbs whose infinitives end with -er make up the most common conjugation in French.

Several categories of -er verbs do not quite conform to this pattern in the present tense:

  • Spelling Changes in the Present Tense for -er Verbs with a Silent e

    For verbs containing a silent e in the last syllable of the verb stem (i.e., the second-to-last syllable of the infinitive), that e becomes è for those forms where this syllable precedes another silent e. This rule is really not so complex as it sounds; in effect, for these verbs e becomes è in all forms of the present tense except for vous and nous: The conjugations of -er verbs with silent e's in the future tense are also affected. The conjugations of -er verbs with silent e's in the conditional tense are also affected. Other verbs which follow these patterns are acheter , achever , élever , geler , mener , peser , and se promener .

  • Spelling Changes for -ler and -ter Verbs in the Present Tense

    For verbs ending with -ler or -ter, the last consonant of the verb stem is doubled before a silent e: -ler and -ter verbs' conjugations in the future tense are also affected. -ler and -ter verbs' conjugations in the conditional tense are also affected. jeter and rappeler are other verbs which follow this pattern.

  • Spelling Changes for -yer Verbs in the Present Tense

    For verbs ending with -yer, the y is replaced by a i before a silent e: -yer verbs' conjugations in the future tense are also affected. Other verbs which follow these patterns are ennuyer , essayer , essuyer , and nettoyer .

  • Spelling Changes for é-er Verbs in the Present Tense

    For -er verbs where the stem's last syllable contains a é, that é is replaced by è in all forms of the present tense except vous and nous: Other verbs which follow this pattern are célébrer , espérer , posséder , préférer , and protéger .

  • Spelling Changes for -ger Verbs in the Present Tense

    For verbs ending with -ger, a silent e is added between the g and any ending beginning with a or o. The way to remember these changes is to think of them as required in order to maintain the soft g that the French ear expects in any form of manger. (In French, any g followed by a or o is pronounced as a hard g (as in gone, as opposed to a soft g, as in George).) In the present tense, the only form which is affected is nous: -ger verbs' conjugations in the imperfect tense are also affected. Other verbs which follow these patterns are charger , corriger , nager , partager , songer , and voyager .

  • Spelling Changes for -cer Verbs in the Present Tense

    For verbs ending with -cer, a silent e is added between the g and any ending beginning with a or o. The way to remember these changes is to think of them as required in order to maintain the soft c that the French ear expects at the end of any form of commencer. (In French, any c followed by a or o is pronounced as a hard c (as in cut, as opposed to a soft c, as in Cyrus).) In the present tense, the only form which is affected is nous: -cer verbs' conjugations in the imperfect tense are also affected. Other verbs which follow these patterns are annoncer , avancer , placer , prononcer , and remplacer .

Negative Statements

To negate a French statement, insert ne before the verb and add pas after it.

Je ne parle pas espagnol.
I do not not speak Spanish.

When ne is followed by a word beginning with a vowel, it becomes n'.

N'allez pas à la plage!
Don't go [familiar, plural] to the beach!

Imperative -- forming commands

Simply omit the personal pronouns to form a command.

Parlez français!
Speak French!

    Note that the trailing s is dropped except before y and en for second person singular imperative forms of -er verbs. =

    Parle français!
    Speak French!

    Pensez vite!
    Think [familiar, plural] quickly!

Forming questions using inversion

Except for the case of the je form, one can form questions in French by inverting the subject and the verb.
  • In cases where the interrogative inversion will lead to consecutive vowels, a t is inserted to separate them.

    parles-tu?
    parle-t-il?
    parle-t-elle?
    parle-t-on?

    parlons-nous?
    parlez-vous?
    parlent-ils?
    parlent-elles?

  • Negative questions can be formed in French by inverting the subject and verb and adding ne and pas.

    ne parles-tu pas?
    ne parle-t-il pas?
    ne parle-t-elle pas?
    ne parle-t-on pas?

    ne parlons-nous pas?
    ne parlez-vous pas?
    ne parlent-ils pas?
    ne parlent-elles pas?

Forming Questions with est-ce que

One can form questions in French by simply prefixing the phrase est-ce que to a statement.

Est-ce qu'il parle?
Is he talking?

est-ce que tu parles?
est-ce qu'il parle?
est-ce qu'elle parle?
est-ce qu'on parle?

est-ce que nous parlons?
est-ce que vous parlez?
est-ce qu'ils parlent?
est-ce qu'elles parlent?

  • In French one uses the word si, not oui, to respond affirmatively to a negative question.

    Ne parles-tu pas français? Si, je parle français.
    Do you [formal, singular] not speak French? Yes, I do speak French.

  • To ask questions about the past, see using the past tense to form questions.

Reflexive Verbs

A reflexive verb expresses an action executed on the verb's subject, which is also represented in the sentence by a reflexive pronoun.

This grammatical style is used only occasionally in English, e.g., he helped himself, but is used much more often in French.

Nous nous amusons beaucoup.
We are enjoying ourselves very much.

Je m'appelle John.
I'm called John.

Je me bats contre l'injustice.
I am fighting against injustice.

Il est possible de se blesser à cet endroit.
It is possible to hurt oneself at that place.

Nous nous brossons les dents chaque nuit avant de nous coucher.
We brush our teeth each night before going to bed.

Dépêchez-vous et déshabillez-vous!
Hurry up and get undressed!

Je m'en vais.
I'm going away.

Parfois il est difficile de s'endormir.
Sometimes it is difficult to fall asleep.

Elle se fâche avec nous.
She is getting angry with us.

Tu devrais t'habiller tout de suite, ma petite fille.
You [informal, singular] should get dressed immediately, my little girl.

Je me lave constamment.
I wash myself constantly.

Malheureusement, ici tout le monde se lève très tôt chaque matin.
Unfortunately, everyone gets up very early each morning here.

Ils se marient.
They're getting married.

Qu'est-ce qui se passe là-bas?
What's happening over there?

Chaque jour qu'il fait du soleil, je me promène.
Everyday that it's sunny, I take a walk.

Je me rappelle même trop bien de cet événement.
I remember that event only too well.

Un homme doit se raser chaque jour.
A man must shave each day.

Je me rends compte que j'ai fait des erreurs.
I realize that I made some mistakes.

Je me repose le dimanche.
I rest on Sundays.

Quand je me réveille tard, je suis plus heureux.

Be quiet!

L'école se trouve à gauche.

La femme à droite reste silencieuse.

le passé composé: Past Tense

Events which took place in the past are described using the past tense. In French this tense is constructed using a helper verb, normally avoir, along with the past participle of the verb describing what happened. This is analogous to the use of the verb to have for the same purpose in English.

Forming the Past Participle

  • You can form the past participle for an -er verb by replacing the -er with é.

    • Il a parlé.
    He has talked.

  • You can form the past participle for an -ir verb by replacing the -ir with i.

    • Ils ont fini.
    They have finished.

  • You can form the past participle for an -re verb by replacing the -re with u.

    • Il a vendu.
    He sold.

  • There is no rule for forming the past participle for irregular verbs. Here the student must simply memorize the various possibilities.

    • verb French past participle English
    avoir eu to have
    finir fini to finish
    parler parlé to speak
    vendre vendu to sell

    Il était sale hier.
    He was dirty yesterday.

    Quelques-uns de mes amis vont souvent à cet endroit.
    Some of my friends often go to this place.

    J'ai couru.
    I ran.

In English it is possible to express the past tense without using a helper verb. This is not possible in French:

Il a fini.
He finished.

Forming the Past Using Être

Although most French verbs use avoir as their helper verb to express the past, a small number of common verbs use être instead. When a compound past tense is formed using être, the past participle is treated like an adjective of the subject, and so must agree with that subject in gender and number.

Ma mère est allée au magasin.
My mother went to the store.

Most, but not all, of the non-reflexive verbs which form the past with être pertain to motion:

Vous êtes allé au coin de la rue.
You [formal, singular] went to the street corner.

Tu seras venu en retard.
You [familiar, singular] will have come late.

Reflexive verbs also use être as their helper verb when composing the past tense.

Je me suis levé ce matin.
I got up this morning.

Direct Objects which Precede the Past Participle

When any direct object(s) precedes the past participle, that past participle is treated as if it were an adjective modifying the direct object(s). Thus the usual rules regarding agreement between adjectives and their objects in gender and number must be observed.

Elle a battu les enfants. Elle les a battus.
She beat the children. She beat them.

Il a soulevé les filles afin de leur permettre de voir. Il les a soulevées.
He lifted the girls up to allow them to see. He lifted them up.

Reflexive pronouns are direct objects. Since the reflexive pronoun always precedes the past participle, the rule just described always applies. In effect this boils down the following rule:
Past tense statements using reflexive verbs require their past participles to agree with their subjects.

Nous nous sommes arrêtés juste à temps.
We stopped ourselves just in time.

Les écolières m'ont dit que «Nous nous sommes arrêtées juste à temps!»
The school girls said to me, “We stopped just in time!”

Negating the Past Tense

To make a negative statement using the past tense, simply negate the helper verb:

Il n'a pas vendu.
He didn't sell.

Nous ne nous sommes pas parlé.
We have not spoken to each other.

Ce garçon-la` ne s'est jamais brossé les dents.
That boy never brushed his teeth.

Asking Questions in the Past Tense

To ask a question using the past tense, simply apply the usual principles for asking questions to the helper verb:

Est-il venu ici?
Did he come here?

A-t-elle bu sa boisson?
Did she drink her drink?

Est-ce que vous vous êtes réveillés?
Have you [formal, singular] woken up?

l'imparfait: Imperfect Tense

The imperfect tense is used in French to describe repeated or extended action in the past. This is in contrast to le passé composé , which is used to describe singular events which occurred in the past.

The imperfect is formed by dropping the -ons ending from the nous form of the present tense and then adding the imperfect endings. All French verbs conjugate the imperfect by dropping the -ons ending from the nous form of the present tense and then adding the imperfect endings except for être, which is, as usual, irregular:

Vous achetiez des livres.
You [formal, plural] used to buy books.

Ils parlaient portugais.
They used to speak Portuguese.

Several categories of -er verbs do not quite conform to the standard pattern of the imperfect tense:

  • Spelling Changes for -ger Verbs in the Imperfect Tense

    For verbs ending with -ger, a silent e is added between the g and any ending beginning with a or o. The way to remember these changes is to think of them as required in order to maintain the soft g that the French ear expects in any form of manger. (In French, any g followed by a or o is pronounced as a hard g (as in gone, as opposed to a soft g, as in George).) In the imperfect tense, all forms except for vous and nous are affected: -ger verbs' conjugations in the present tense are also affected. Other verbs which follow these patterns are charger , corriger , nager , partager , songer , and voyager .

  • Spelling Changes for -cer Verbs in the Imperfect Tense

    For verbs ending with -cer, a silent e is added between the g and any ending beginning with a or o. The way to remember these changes is to think of them as required in order to maintain the soft c that the French ear expects at the end of any form of commencer. (In French, any c followed by a or o is pronounced as a hard c (as in cut, as opposed to a soft g, as in Cyrus).) In the imperfect tense, all forms except for vous and nous are affected: -cer verbs' conjugations in the present tense are also affected. Other verbs which follow these patterns are annoncer , avancer , placer , prononcer , and remplacer .

le futur antérieur: Future Perfect Tense

The future perfect tense is used to describe events which will have happened in the future. It is formed by conjugating the auxiliary verb in the future tense, and adding the past participle of the action verb.

le plus-que-parfait: Pluperfect Tense

The pluperfect tense is used to describe events which had happened in the past. It is formed by conjugating the auxiliary verb in the imperfect tense, and adding the past participle of the action verb.

le subjonctif: Subjunctive Mood

The subjunctive mood is frequently a problem area for English speakers because its use in French generally does not have a direct equivalent in English. It is used to express doubt and emotion, but it is probably best for the student to initially focus less on precisely translating its meaning than on developing a sense for the various situations in French where its use is obligatory. Below are some of these situations:
  • The subjunctive mood is required in dependent clauses where the main clause expresses a desire.

    J'aimerais qu'elle soit ici.
    I wish that she were here.

    Je veux qu'elle sache que je l'ai dupée.
    I want her to know that I tricked her.

  • The subjunctive mood is required in dependent clauses where the main clause expresses a command.

    J'ai ordonné que ce crime aie lieu.
    I commanded that this crime take place.

  • The subjunctive mood is required in dependent clauses where the main clause expresses a doubt or denial.

    Nous doutons que nous puissions le faire.
    We doubt that we can do it.

    Elle nie que son mari soit laid.
    She denies that her husband is ugly.

  • The subjunctive mood is required in dependent clauses where the main clause expresses fear.

    Ils craignent que le gosse ne se perde.
    They fear that the boy will get lost.

  • The subjunctive mood is required in dependent clauses where the main clause expresses emotions like joy or sorrow.

    Je suis tellement content qu'ils soient morts!
    I am so happy that they are dead!

  • The subjunctive mood is required in dependent clauses where the main clause expresses a hope.

    Nous espérons tous que cet homme parte.
    We all hope that this man leaves.

  • The subjunctive mood is required in dependent clauses where the main clause expresses a superlative statement.

    C'est la dernière fois que vous me voyiez!
    This is the last time that you [singular, formal] will see me!

    C'est la plus belle femme que j'aie jamais vue!
    That's the most beautiful woman I have ever seen!

  • The subjunctive mood is required in dependent clauses where the main clause is an impersonal expression expressing uncertainty, necessity, or appropriateness.

    Il faut que tu viennes.
    You [formal, singular] must come.

    Il vaut mieux qu'il ne sache pas.
    It is better that he doesn't know.

    Il est temps que nous fassions de notre mieux.
    It is time that we tried our best.

    Il est possible que nous venions ce soir.
    It is possible that we come this evening.

    Il est juste qu'il admette ses erreurs.
    It is right that he admit his errors.

    Il semble que nous soyons les victimes.
    It seems that we are the victims.

    Il est rare qu'on voie un tel oiseau ici.
    It is rare that one sees such a bird here.

    Il est bon que nous arrivions si tôt.
    It is good that we arrive so early.

    It is unfortunate that we are not more intelligent.

    Il est important qu'elle puisse apprendre à vivre seule.
    It is important that she be able to learn to live alone.

    But:
    The indicative, not the subjunctive, is appropriate in dependent clauses where the main clause is an impersonal expression expressing certainty or substantial probability.

    Il est clair qu'il est stupide.
    It is clear that he is stupid.

    Il est sûr que nous arriverons à temps.
    It is certain that we will arrive in time.

    Dependent clauses where the main clause is an impersonal expression expressing certainty or substantial probability require the subjunctive if the statement is negative or interrogative.

    Est-il clair qu'il soit stupide?
    Is it clear that he is stupid?

    Est-ce qu'il est sûr que nous arrivions à temps?
    Is it certain that we will arrive in time?

  • The subjunctive mood is required in relative clauses where the existence or identity of the antecedent of the relative pronoun is not certain or is not known precisely.

    Nous ne connaissons personne qui puisse nous aider.
    We don't know anyone who can help us.

  • The subjunctive mood is required in dependent clauses following certain conjunctive phrases.

    Il continuera de dormir jusqu'à ce que nous arrivions là-bas.
    He will continue to sleep until we get there.

  • The subjunctive is obligatory in independent clauses expressing a command in the third person. Normally this style is the province of people blessed with tremendous authority, e.g., kings.

    Qu'ils se taisent!
    Let them be quiet!

Note that the subjunctive does not change form to refer to future events.

Tout le monde a peur que le monstre revienne.
Everyone is afraid that the monster will come again.

The past conditional tense is used to describe events which would have happened in the past. It is formed by conjugating the auxiliary verb in the conditional tense, and adding the past participle of the action verb.

The passé simple is used in formal writing to describe an event which occurred at a particular time in the past. Note that French people don't use this tense in spoken conversation; instead they use the passé composé.

Words Starting with Vowels and Silent h's

If a word begins with a vowel or an unaspirated (i.e., silent) h, then it combines with de, le, la, or ne. If a word begins with an aspirated h, then it does not combine with de, le, la, or ne. Unfortunately, there is no way to tell by inspection whether an h is aspirated or not. Better French dictionaries note whether leading h's are pronounced or not (e.g., in Cassell's dictionaries, pronounced h's are preceded by the † dagger character).

Vous peindrez le hangar.
You [familiar, plural] will paint the shed.

Objects

When le is used as an object pronoun, it is never combined with de to form the contraction du. In contrast, when le is used as a definite article, it always combines with a preceding de to form du (cf. prepositions). Similarly, les doesn't combine with de to create the contraction des when les is being used as an object pronoun.

Forming Plurals

Most French nouns and adjectives form the plural by simply adding an -s to the end. But there are several widespread classes of exceptions to this rule:
  • French nouns ending with -au, -eau or -eu add an -x to the end to form the plural.
  • French nouns ending with -s, -x, or -z do not change at all in the plural.
  • French nouns ending with -al or -ail form the plural by adopting the ending -aux.
  • Some French nouns ending with ou formed the plural by adding -x.
  • Compound nouns composed of two nouns or a noun and an adjective joined by a hyphen are made plural by making each of the component words plural.
  • Proper nouns do not change in their plural forms.
  • Compound nouns composed by a pair of nouns joined by a proposition are made plural by making the first noun plural.
  • Compound nouns composed by a noun and one of either a verb, adverb or proposition are made plural by making just the noun plural.
  • Compound nouns composed by a pair of verbs do not change in their plural forms.
And naturally there exist some French nouns which form irregular plurals.

In French adjectives do not have a single form, as they do in English. French adjectives must agree with their objects in gender and number.

Places

To drill yourself on the names of countries and cities, go to geography drills

In French the rules governing the treatment of place names are quite different from those in English.
  • Several cities are always referred to in French with the definite article (although the great majority are not).

    Nous voyagions à la Havane.
    We used to travel to Havana.

  • A definite article is required in French when referring to countries, regions, continents, mountains, and rivers.
  • In French one uses à to express in or to a city.

    Il voyage à Rome.
    He travels to Rome.

  • In French one uses à and the definite article (i.e., au, aux, or à l') to express in or to an unmodified masculine country.

    Ils voyagent en Chine.
    They travel to China.

  • In French one uses en to express in or to an unmodified feminine country.

    Vous voyagez en Espagne.
    You [familiar, plural] travel to Spain.

  • In French one uses dans to express in or to a country modified by an adjective.

    Je voyageais en Corée du Sud.
    I used to travel to South Korea.

  • In French one uses de to express from a feminine country that is not modified by an adjective.

    Ne voyage pas en France!
    Don't travel [familiar, singular] to France!

  • In French one uses de and the appropriate determiner to express from any masculine country, and also feminine countries which are modified by adjectives.

    Vous voyagez au Japon.
    You [formal, singular] travel to Japan.
